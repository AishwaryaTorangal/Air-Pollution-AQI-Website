What is Air Quality Index (AQI)?

The Air Quality Index (AQI) is a measurement used by government agencies to communicate to the public how polluted the air currently is or how polluted it is forecast to become. The AQI focuses on health effects you may experience within a few hours or days after breathing polluted air.

AQI values are calculated for major air pollutants including:

  • Ground-level ozone (O3)
  • Particle pollution (PM2.5 and PM10)
  • Carbon monoxide (CO)
  • Sulfur dioxide (SO2)
  • Nitrogen dioxide (NO2)

AQI Levels and Health Implications

Good (0-50)

Health Implications

Air quality is considered satisfactory, and air pollution poses little or no risk.

Precautionary Actions

  • No health impacts are expected
  • Ideal for outdoor activities

Moderate (51-100)

Health Implications

Air quality is acceptable; however, there may be a risk for some people who are unusually sensitive to air pollution.

Precautionary Actions

  • Unusually sensitive people should consider reducing prolonged or heavy outdoor exertion

Unhealthy for Sensitive Groups (101-150)

Health Implications

Members of sensitive groups may experience health effects. The general public is less likely to be affected.

Precautionary Actions

  • Sensitive groups should reduce prolonged or heavy outdoor exertion
  • People with heart or lung disease, older adults, and children should limit outdoor activities

Unhealthy (151-200)

Health Implications

Some members of the general public may experience health effects; members of sensitive groups may experience more serious health effects.

Precautionary Actions

  • Everyone should reduce prolonged or heavy outdoor exertion
  • Sensitive groups should avoid prolonged or heavy outdoor activities

Very Unhealthy (201-300)

Health Implications

Health alert: The risk of health effects is increased for everyone.

Precautionary Actions

  • Everyone should avoid prolonged or heavy outdoor activities
  • Sensitive groups should avoid all outdoor physical activity
  • Keep windows closed and use air purifiers if available

Hazardous (301+)

Health Implications

Health warning of emergency conditions: everyone is more likely to be affected.

Precautionary Actions

  • Everyone should avoid all outdoor activities
  • Remain indoors and keep activity levels low
  • Use air purifiers and keep windows closed
  • Follow advice from local authorities

Major Air Pollutants

PM2.5 (Fine Particulate Matter)

Particles smaller than 2.5 micrometers that can penetrate deep into the lungs and enter the bloodstream. Sources include vehicle emissions, power plants, and wildfires.

Health Effects: Respiratory and cardiovascular problems, aggravated asthma, decreased lung function.

PM10 (Coarse Particulate Matter)

Particles smaller than 10 micrometers that can be inhaled. Sources include dust, pollen, mold, and construction sites.

Health Effects: Eye, nose, and throat irritation, aggravated asthma, bronchitis.

Ozone (O3)

A gas created by chemical reactions between oxides of nitrogen and volatile organic compounds in the presence of sunlight.

Health Effects: Chest pain, coughing, throat irritation, worsened bronchitis, emphysema, and asthma.

Nitrogen Dioxide (NO2)

A reddish-brown gas with a sharp odor, primarily formed from high-temperature combustion.

Health Effects: Respiratory problems, increased asthma symptoms, inflammation of airways.

Sulfur Dioxide (SO2)

A colorless gas with a sharp odor, produced from burning fossil fuels containing sulfur.

Health Effects: Respiratory problems, eye irritation, aggravated asthma and bronchitis.

Carbon Monoxide (CO)

A colorless, odorless gas produced by incomplete combustion of carbon-containing fuels.

Health Effects: Reduced oxygen delivery to organs, dizziness, confusion, unconsciousness, and at high levels, death.
